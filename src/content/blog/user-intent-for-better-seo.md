User intent is the practical reason behind a search. It answers the question, "What does the searcher need right now?" If your page misses that job, better optimization will not rescue it.

## The four intent buckets still matter

The classic model is still useful:

- informational
- navigational
- commercial investigation
- transactional

## Why intent matters more in AI-era search

Answer systems compress the distance between query and response. That means intent mismatch becomes obvious faster. A page written like a long thought piece will not satisfy a user who wants a quick comparison. A thin definition page will not satisfy someone ready to evaluate tools.

## Practical intent matching

Ask these questions before drafting:

1. What is the user trying to understand?
2. What decision are they trying to make?
3. How quickly do they need the answer?
4. What proof would reduce uncertainty?

Those questions lead to much better page structures.

## Intent-led page design examples

### Informational

Lead with a definition, then expand with examples and frameworks.

### Commercial investigation

Lead with options, criteria, and tradeoffs.

### Transactional

Lead with the offer, friction reducers, and next action.

Every page should have one dominant intent, even if it touches adjacent needs. Mixed-intent pages often feel comprehensive, but they usually convert poorly because they never commit to the user’s actual job.
